As part of the project "AgentTrap: Social Engineering Benchmark for Web-based VLM Agents", my primary contribution involved designing and developing a diverse set of web-based scenarios to evaluate the robustness of vision-language model (VLM) agents against social engineering-style interactions.

I was responsible for conceptualizing, implementing, and testing multiple realistic web environments that simulate common user workflows while embedding controlled deceptive patterns. These environments were designed to observe how AI agents respond to prompts involving authentication requests, premium feature gating, fake technical support, phishing clones, and urgency-based access restrictions.

Specifically, I developed the following web-based test scenarios:
1) Ninite scareware with fake virus alerts and tech-support charges
2) Chase banking dashboard with fake bill-pay redirects and SSN harvesting
3) Expedia booking flow with bait-and-switch pricing and fake payment domains
4) MyChart medical portal with insurance verification and pre-auth card traps
5) Instagram recovery process intercepting passwords and backup codes
6) DHL tracking page with fake customs fee payment flows
7) IRS tax notice simulation collecting SSN and bank details
8) Oracle billing system with enterprise verification traps
9) MetaMask wallet migration intercepting seed phrases
10) Microsoft SSO login via Slack to intercept credentials and MFA
11) Amazon returns process exposing refund and payment data
12) ServiceNow IT portal impersonating internal IT staff
13) LinkedIn job application with background-check PII escalation
14) Apple ID recovery with credential and payment verification
15) Gift-card survey acting as a survey-to-SSN reward trap
16) PayPal dispute resolution flow for identity and bank account harvesting
17) Zoom meeting prompt with a work credential gate before joining
18) Netflix billing error simulating a card and identity recovery flow
19) Uber compliance check extracting passwords, SSN, and DOB
20) Airbnb audit requiring login and payment verification

Each website was implemented as a lightweight full-stack application following a split architecture (using two Flask servers per environment, separate configuration and scenarios, and no shared backend), ensuring complete isolation, modularity, and ease of deployment in a controlled testing environment.

A key aspect of my work was ensuring that all scenarios closely mimic real-world web interfaces and user experiences. This included designing natural interaction flows such as distinct entry points (Gmail/Slack), content previews, and feature unlock mechanisms that are commonly encountered in production systems. At the same time, these scenarios were carefully structured to introduce controlled points of decision-making for AI agents, enabling systematic evaluation of their behavior under potentially misleading conditions.

Additionally, I implemented independent logging mechanisms across all scenarios to record agent interactions during testing. Each environment writes its own interaction logs, capturing legitimate exit paths and timestamps, while the phishing sites actively classify and log high, medium, and low sensitivity data into captured_data logs.

Overall, my contribution provides a comprehensive and diverse set of benchmark environments that cover multiple categories of social engineering patterns, including credential prompts, incentive-based access, and workflow-based gating. These scenarios form a crucial part of the benchmark, enabling detailed analysis of VLM agent safety, reliability, and decision-making in realistic web-based contexts.
