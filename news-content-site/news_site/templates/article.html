AI Regulation: What the New EU Framework Means for Tech Companies

The European Union's landmark legislation imposes sweeping new requirements on artificial intelligence systems, from hiring algorithms to chatbots. Companies have 18 months to comply — or face fines of up to 7 percent of global revenue.

By Catherine A. Mercer and Jonathan Liu Catherine Mercer covers technology policy from Brussels. Jonathan Liu reports on artificial intelligence from San Francisco.
Published March 15, 2025 · Updated March 17, 2025
Thierry Monasse / Getty Images

Members of the European Parliament during the final vote on the AI Governance Act in Strasbourg, France, on Thursday.

BRUSSELS — The European Union on Thursday adopted the most far-reaching set of regulations governing artificial intelligence anywhere in the world, a sweeping legislative package that will reshape how technology companies build, deploy and market AI systems across the 27-nation bloc.

The AI Governance Act, which passed with a decisive 412-to-89 majority in the European Parliament, establishes a tiered risk framework that classifies AI applications according to their potential for harm. Systems deemed "high risk" — including those used in hiring, credit scoring, law enforcement and critical infrastructure — will face stringent transparency requirements, mandatory human oversight and regular third-party audits.

"This is not about stifling innovation," said Margarethe Halversen, the Danish politician who shepherded the legislation through three years of negotiations. "It is about ensuring that artificial intelligence serves European citizens rather than exploiting them. The era of unregulated AI experimentation is over."

The legislation arrives at a moment of extraordinary tension between governments and the technology industry. Over the past two years, the rapid proliferation of generative AI tools — from chatbots capable of producing human-quality text to systems that can generate photorealistic images — has created an atmosphere of urgency among regulators worldwide.

Under the new framework, companies that deploy AI systems in the European Union will be required to maintain detailed documentation of their training data, conduct impact assessments before deployment and provide clear disclosure to users when they are interacting with an AI system. The requirements apply regardless of where the company is headquartered, a provision that effectively extends the law's reach to American and Chinese technology firms operating in Europe.

A Global Ripple Effect

Industry analysts say the regulations are likely to have effects far beyond Europe's borders, much as the General Data Protection Regulation, or GDPR, became a de facto global privacy standard after its adoption in 2018.

"Companies are not going to build two versions of every product," said Dr. Elena Vasquez, a professor of technology policy at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. "What Europe mandates, the world inherits. We saw it with data privacy, and we'll see it with AI governance."

The comparison to GDPR is apt, though the new law goes further in several respects. While GDPR focused primarily on data collection and storage, the AI Governance Act reaches into the algorithmic decision-making processes themselves, requiring companies to demonstrate that their systems do not produce discriminatory outcomes and that affected individuals have meaningful avenues for redress.

Major technology companies have offered cautious responses. In a statement, a Google spokesperson said the company "supports thoughtful AI regulation and will work to comply with the new requirements." Meta declined to comment. OpenAI, whose ChatGPT chatbot has become the public face of generative AI, said it was "reviewing the legislation's implications for our European operations."

The Compliance Challenge

For smaller companies, however, the law presents a more daunting challenge. The compliance requirements — particularly the mandates for algorithmic auditing and transparency documentation — will require significant technical and legal resources that many startups may struggle to afford.

"There is a real risk that this becomes a moat for incumbents," said James Park, the chief executive of Meridian AI, a Berlin-based startup that builds hiring tools for small businesses. "Google and Microsoft can afford compliance departments. A 20-person startup in Lisbon cannot. The question is whether the exemptions for small enterprises go far enough."

The law does include scaled exemptions for companies with fewer than 50 employees and annual revenue below €10 million, allowing them extended timelines and reduced documentation requirements. But critics argue these thresholds are too low and will still catch many growth-stage companies.

Perhaps the most contentious provision is the requirement for "algorithmic transparency" — the obligation to explain, in terms accessible to non-experts, how an AI system reached a particular decision. For systems built on large language models, which operate through complex statistical patterns learned from vast training datasets, this presents a fundamental technical challenge.

Enforcement and Penalties

"You cannot meaningfully explain why a model with 400 billion parameters produced a specific output," said Dr. Raj Patel, head of AI research at the Technical University of Munich. "The law is asking for something that the current state of the science cannot fully provide. We can offer approximations, feature attributions, confidence intervals — but true explanations remain an open research problem."

Regulators appear aware of this tension. The legislation includes a provision for the creation of a European AI Standards Board, which will develop technical guidelines for compliance. Those guidelines, which are expected to take 12 to 18 months to finalize, will effectively determine how strictly the transparency requirements are interpreted.

The penalties for noncompliance are substantial. Companies that violate the core provisions can face fines of up to 7 percent of global annual revenue — exceeding even the 4 percent maximum under GDPR. For a company like Apple, with annual revenue exceeding $380 billion, this could translate to a theoretical maximum fine of more than $26 billion.

Enforcement will be handled by a combination of national regulatory authorities and a new centralized body, the European AI Office, based in Brussels. The office will have the authority to conduct investigations, issue binding decisions and coordinate enforcement actions across member states.

What Comes Next

The United States has so far taken a markedly different approach, relying primarily on voluntary commitments from AI companies and sector-specific guidance from existing regulatory agencies. President Biden's executive order on AI, issued in October 2023, established reporting requirements for developers of powerful AI systems but stopped well short of the comprehensive regulatory framework Europe has now adopted.

Senator Maria Caldwell, who chairs the Senate Commerce Committee, said Thursday that the European vote should serve as "a wake-up call" for American lawmakers. "We cannot outsource our AI governance to Brussels," she said. "The United States needs its own legislative framework, one that protects our citizens while preserving our competitive edge in AI development."

For now, however, the European Union has established itself as the undisputed global leader in AI regulation. The question that remains — one that will likely take years to answer definitively — is whether that leadership will ultimately prove to be a competitive advantage or a burden.
